All Mental Health Disorders

A comprehensive, compassionate guide to every recognised mental health disorder based on DSM-5-TR and ICD-11. Understanding is the beginning of healing.

This page is educational, not diagnostic. Always consult a qualified professional.

Neurodevelopmental Disorders

Conditions with onset in early development, affecting brain function, learning, and behaviour.

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

Neurodevelopmental

A developmental condition characterised by differences in social communication, social interaction, and restricted or repetitive patterns of behaviour and interests. It is a spectrum no two people experience it the same way.

Key Features
Social communication differencesRepetitive behavioursSensory sensitivitiesRestricted interests

ADHD — Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder

Neurodevelopmental

A pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functioning or development. Exists in three presentations: predominantly inattentive, predominantly hyperactive-impulsive, or combined.

Key Features
InattentionHyperactivityImpulsivityDifficulty organising

Intellectual Disability

Neurodevelopmental

Significant limitations in both intellectual functioning (reasoning, problem-solving, planning) and adaptive behaviour (practical, social, and conceptual skills), originating during the developmental period.

Key Features
Low IQAdaptive skill deficitsCommunication difficulties

Specific Learning Disorder

Neurodevelopmental

Persistent difficulties in learning academic skills including reading (dyslexia), written expression (dysgraphia), or mathematics (dyscalculia) that are not explained by intellectual disability or inadequate instruction.

Key Features
DyslexiaDyscalculiaDysgraphiaSlow academic progress

Communication Disorders

Neurodevelopmental

A group of disorders including Language Disorder, Speech Sound Disorder, Childhood-Onset Fluency Disorder (stuttering), and Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder affecting how a person understands and uses language.

Key Features
StutteringLimited vocabularySpeech sound errors

Developmental Coordination Disorder (Dyspraxia)

Neurodevelopmental

Acquisition and execution of coordinated motor skills is substantially below expected levels. Affects daily activities like tying shoelaces, handwriting, sports, and using tools.

Key Features
Poor motor coordinationClumsinessSlow motor learning

Tourette's Syndrome & Tic Disorders

Neurodevelopmental

Repetitive, involuntary movements and vocalisations called tics. Tourette's involves both motor and vocal tics for more than a year. Other tic disorders include Persistent Motor or Vocal Tic Disorder and Provisional Tic Disorder.

Key Features
Motor ticsVocal ticsUrge to ticFluctuating severity

Schizophrenia Spectrum & Psychotic Disorders

Disorders characterised by delusions, hallucinations, disorganised thinking, and disturbances in perception of reality.

Schizophrenia

Psychotic

A chronic condition involving disruptions in thinking, perception, emotions, and behaviour. Positive symptoms include hallucinations and delusions; negative symptoms include flat affect, social withdrawal, and lack of motivation.

Key Features
HallucinationsDelusionsDisorganised speechFlat affectAvolition

Schizoaffective Disorder

Psychotic

A combination of schizophrenia symptoms (hallucinations, delusions) and mood disorder symptoms (major depressive or manic episodes). Exists in depressive type and bipolar type.

Key Features
Psychotic symptomsMood episodesFunctional impairment

Delusional Disorder

Psychotic

Presence of one or more fixed, false beliefs (delusions) for at least one month, without the other symptoms of schizophrenia. Types include persecutory, grandiose, erotomanic, jealous, somatic, and mixed.

Key Features
Non-bizarre delusionsRelatively preserved functioning

Brief Psychotic Disorder

Psychotic

Sudden onset of at least one psychotic symptom (delusions, hallucinations, or disorganised speech) lasting at least one day but less than one month, with eventual full return to previous functioning.

Key Features
Sudden onsetShort durationFull recovery

Schizophreniform Disorder

Psychotic

Symptoms identical to schizophrenia but lasting between one and six months. May resolve completely or progress to schizophrenia.

Key Features
Psychotic symptoms1–6 month duration

Shared Psychotic Disorder (Folie à Deux)

Psychotic

Delusions that develop in an individual in the context of a close relationship with another person who already has a psychotic disorder. The shared beliefs are often persecutory.

Key Features
Shared delusionsClose relationship

Substance/Medication-Induced Psychotic Disorder

Psychotic

Hallucinations or delusions caused directly by intoxication or withdrawal from substances like alcohol, cannabis, stimulants, or hallucinogens, or from medications.

Key Features
Substance-related onsetHallucinationsDelusions

Bipolar & Depressive (Mood) Disorders

Disorders involving abnormal mood states — depression, mania, hypomania — that significantly affect quality of life.

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

Depressive

Persistent low mood, loss of interest or pleasure in activities, and a range of emotional and physical symptoms that impair daily functioning. One of the most common mental health conditions globally.

Key Features
Persistent sadnessLoss of interestFatigueSleep changesSuicidal thoughts

Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)

Depressive

Chronic, low-grade depression lasting at least two years (one year in children and adolescents). Less severe than major depression but significantly impacts quality of life over the long term.

Key Features
Chronic low moodLow energyPoor self-esteemHopelessness

Bipolar I Disorder

Bipolar

Defined by at least one manic episode lasting at least 7 days (or shorter if hospitalisation is required). Depressive episodes typically also occur. Mania can include elevated mood, grandiosity, decreased need for sleep, reckless behaviour, and psychosis.

Key Features
Manic episodesDepressive episodesGrandiosityDecreased sleepReckless behaviour

Bipolar II Disorder

Bipolar

At least one hypomanic episode and at least one major depressive episode. No full manic episodes. Often misdiagnosed as depression because the hypomanic periods may feel productive.

Key Features
HypomaniaMajor depressionNo full mania

Cyclothymic Disorder

Bipolar

Chronic, fluctuating mood disturbance involving numerous periods of hypomanic and depressive symptoms that do not meet full criteria for hypomanic or major depressive episodes, lasting at least 2 years.

Key Features
Mood swingsChronic 2+ yearsSub-threshold symptoms

Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD)

Depressive

Severe mood, behavioural, and physical symptoms in the week before menstruation that improve after the period starts. More severe than PMS and significantly disrupts daily functioning.

Key Features
Mood labilityIrritabilityDepressed moodCyclical pattern

Disruptive Mood Dysregulation Disorder (DMDD)

Depressive

Characterised by severe, recurrent temper outbursts that are grossly disproportionate to situations, and persistent irritable or angry mood between outbursts in children and adolescents up to age 18.

Key Features
Severe temper outburstsChronic irritabilityChildren 6–18

Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD)

Depressive

A recurrent pattern of major depressive episodes tied to particular seasons most commonly starting in autumn/winter and remitting in spring. Less common summer-pattern SAD also exists.

Key Features
Seasonal patternHypersomniaCarbohydrate cravingsWeight gain

Postpartum/Perinatal Depression

Depressive

Major depressive episode with onset during pregnancy or within 4 weeks after delivery. Affects both mothers and fathers. More severe than the typical "baby blues" and requires professional treatment.

Key Features
Depressed moodInability to bondExtreme anxietyPostpartum onset

Anxiety & Fear-Related Disorders

Disorders characterised by excessive fear, anxiety, and related behavioural disturbances.

Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Anxiety

Excessive, difficult-to-control worry about multiple different topics (health, finances, work, relationships) occurring more days than not for at least 6 months, accompanied by physical symptoms.

Key Features
Excessive worryMuscle tensionSleep disturbanceRestlessnessFatigue

Panic Disorder

Anxiety

Recurrent, unexpected panic attacks sudden surges of intense fear with physical symptoms such as racing heart, shortness of breath, chest pain, dizziness, and fear of dying or losing control. Followed by persistent concern about future attacks.

Key Features
Panic attacksAnticipatory anxietyAvoidance behaviour

Agoraphobia

Anxiety

Intense fear and avoidance of situations where escape might be difficult or help unavailable such as crowds, public transport, open spaces, or being outside alone. Often develops after panic attacks.

Key Features
Fear of crowdsAvoidanceHomebound in severe cases

Social Anxiety Disorder (Social Phobia)

Anxiety

Marked fear of social situations where the person may be scrutinised, judged negatively, or embarrassed. Leads to significant avoidance of social interactions, affecting relationships, education, and work.

Key Features
Fear of judgmentSocial avoidanceBlushing/sweatingPerformance anxiety

Specific Phobia

Anxiety

Intense, irrational fear of a specific object or situation such as animals, heights, flying, blood, injections, thunder, or enclosed spaces — that is out of proportion to actual danger and leads to avoidance.

Key Features
Specific triggerImmediate fear responseAvoidance

Separation Anxiety Disorder

Anxiety

Excessive fear or anxiety about separation from attachment figures, beyond what is developmentally expected. Can affect children and adults, causing distress, sleep problems, and somatic complaints when separated.

Key Features
Fear of separationNightmares about separationSomatic complaints

Selective Mutism

Anxiety

Consistent failure to speak in specific social situations (e.g. school) despite speaking in other contexts (e.g. at home). Often related to social anxiety and can significantly impact education and social development.

Key Features
No speech in some contextsSpeaks in othersChildhood onset

Obsessive-Compulsive & Related Disorders

Disorders characterised by intrusive thoughts and repetitive behaviours or mental acts aimed at reducing anxiety.

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

OCD-Related

Recurring, unwanted obsessions (intrusive thoughts, images, or urges) and/or compulsions (repetitive behaviours or mental acts) performed to reduce distress. Common themes: contamination, harm, symmetry, forbidden thoughts.

Key Features
ObsessionsCompulsionsEgo-dystonicTime-consuming

Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD)

OCD-Related

Preoccupation with one or more perceived defects in physical appearance that are not observable or appear slight to others. Causes significant distress and repetitive behaviours like mirror checking, reassurance-seeking, or camouflaging.

Key Features
Appearance preoccupationMirror checkingSignificant distress

Hoarding Disorder

OCD-Related

Persistent difficulty discarding possessions, regardless of their actual value, due to distress associated with discarding them. Results in clutter that compromises living spaces and impairs functioning.

Key Features
Difficulty discardingExcessive accumulationCluttered living spaces

Trichotillomania (Hair-Pulling Disorder)

OCD-Related

Recurrent, compulsive urges to pull out hair from the scalp, eyebrows, eyelashes, or other areas of the body, resulting in hair loss and significant distress or impairment.

Key Features
Hair pullingHair lossTension before pullingRelief after pulling

Excoriation (Skin-Picking) Disorder

OCD-Related

Recurrent picking of one's own skin, leading to skin lesions, wounds, and significant distress or impairment. Despite repeated attempts to stop, the behaviour persists.

Key Features
Skin pickingSkin lesionsAttempts to stop

Olfactory Reference Disorder

OCD-Related

Persistent preoccupation with the belief that one emits a foul or unpleasant body odour that is not detectable by others. Causes significant shame, embarrassment, and social avoidance.

Key Features
Smell preoccupationSocial avoidanceChecking behaviour

Trauma- & Stressor-Related Disorders

Disorders definitionally associated with exposure to a traumatic or stressful event.

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

Trauma-Related

Develops after exposure to a traumatic event. Involves intrusive re-experiencing (flashbacks, nightmares), persistent avoidance of trauma reminders, negative alterations in mood and cognition, and hyperarousal.

Key Features
FlashbacksNightmaresHypervigilanceAvoidanceEmotional numbing

Complex PTSD (C-PTSD)

Trauma-Related

Develops after prolonged, repeated trauma such as childhood abuse, domestic violence, or captivity. In addition to PTSD symptoms, includes severe difficulties in emotion regulation, identity, and relationships.

Key Features
PTSD symptomsEmotion dysregulationNegative self-conceptRelationship difficulties

Acute Stress Disorder

Trauma-Related

Occurs within 3 days to 1 month after exposure to a traumatic event and involves intrusion symptoms, negative mood, dissociation, avoidance, and arousal. Often precedes PTSD if untreated.

Key Features
3 days to 1 monthDissociationIntrusion

Adjustment Disorder

Stress-Related

Emotional or behavioural symptoms in response to an identifiable stressor (divorce, job loss, illness) that are disproportionate and cause significant distress. Resolves within 6 months after the stressor ends.

Key Features
Identifiable stressorDepression/anxietyBehavioural changes

Prolonged Grief Disorder

Stress-Related

Intense yearning and longing for the deceased, difficulty accepting the death, bitterness about the loss, and inability to engage in life persisting more than 12 months after bereavement (6 months in ICD-11).

Key Features
Intense yearningGrief beyond 12 monthsFunctional impairment

Reactive Attachment Disorder

Trauma-Related

A pattern of emotionally withdrawn behaviour toward caregivers in children following severe social neglect or deprivation. The child rarely seeks comfort from caregivers and shows minimal positive emotions.

Key Features
Withdrawn from caregiversSocial neglect historyLimited positive affect

Disinhibited Social Engagement Disorder

Trauma-Related

A pattern of behaviour in children involving culturally inappropriate, overly familiar behaviour with relative strangers willingness to go off with strangers, overly affectionate following early social neglect.

Key Features
Indiscriminate approachNo hesitation with strangersNeglect history

Dissociative Disorders

Disruptions in consciousness, memory, identity, emotion, perception, behaviour, and sense of self.

Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID)

Dissociative

Disruption of identity characterised by two or more distinct personality states or "alters," each with their own perspective, way of relating, and sense of self. Associated with severe early childhood trauma. Formerly called Multiple Personality Disorder.

Key Features
Multiple identity statesMemory gapsTrauma historyIdentity disruption

Dissociative Amnesia

Dissociative

Inability to recall important autobiographical information usually traumatic or stressful that is too extensive to be explained by ordinary forgetting. May include dissociative fugue (purposeful travel with memory loss).

Key Features
Memory gapsTrauma-relatedNot organic

Depersonalisation/Derealisation Disorder

Dissociative

Persistent or recurrent experiences of feeling detached from one's mental processes or body (depersonalisation) or feeling as if one is an outside observer of one's surroundings (derealisation). Reality testing remains intact.

Key Features
Feeling unrealDetached from bodyIntact reality testing

Somatic Symptom & Bodily Distress Disorders

Disorders involving significant focus on physical symptoms causing distress or functional disruption.

Somatic Symptom Disorder

Somatic

One or more somatic symptoms causing distress or disruption, accompanied by excessive thoughts, feelings, or behaviours related to the symptoms or associated health concerns that are disproportionate and persistent.

Key Features
Physical symptomsExcessive health anxietyDisproportionate concern

Illness Anxiety Disorder (Health Anxiety)

Somatic

Preoccupation with having or acquiring a serious illness, despite little or no somatic symptoms. Excessive health-related behaviours (frequent checking, doctor visits) or maladaptive avoidance of medical care.

Key Features
Disease preoccupationMinimal symptomsDoctor shopping

Functional Neurological Symptom Disorder (Conversion Disorder)

Somatic

Neurological symptoms such as weakness, paralysis, tremor, non-epileptic seizures, vision or speech problems that cannot be explained by neurological disease. Evidence of incompatibility with neurological conditions.

Key Features
Neurological symptomsNo organic causeMotor/sensory symptoms

Factitious Disorder (Munchausen Syndrome)

Somatic

Falsification of physical or psychological symptoms, or induction of injury or disease, associated with identified deception even without obvious external reward. Can be imposed on oneself or on another person.

Key Features
Deliberate symptom fabricationDeceptionMedical seeking

Psychological Factors Affecting Other Medical Conditions

Somatic

A medical symptom or condition is adversely affected by psychological or behavioural factors — such as anxiety worsening asthma, or stress delaying wound healing. The psychological factors are clinically significant.

Key Features
Medical conditionPsychological influenceAdverse health outcomes

Feeding & Eating Disorders

Persistent disturbances in eating behaviour with harmful consequences to physical and mental health.

Anorexia Nervosa

Eating

Restriction of food intake leading to significantly low body weight, intense fear of weight gain, and disturbed body image. Highest mortality rate of any psychiatric condition. Exists in restricting and binge-purge subtypes.

Key Features
Food restrictionLow body weightDistorted body imageFear of weight gain

Bulimia Nervosa

Eating

Recurrent episodes of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviours (purging, laxative use, excessive exercise) to prevent weight gain. Self-evaluation is unduly influenced by body shape and weight.

Key Features
Binge eatingPurgingCompensatory behaviourGuilt/shame

Binge Eating Disorder (BED)

Eating

Recurrent episodes of consuming large quantities of food in a short period, accompanied by a sense of loss of control and marked distress. Unlike bulimia, there are no regular compensatory behaviours.

Key Features
Binge episodesLoss of controlNo purgingDistress

Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder (ARFID)

Eating

Restriction of food intake based on sensory characteristics, fear of aversive consequences (choking, vomiting), or apparent lack of interest in eating not related to body image concerns, causing nutritional deficiency.

Key Features
Food restrictionSensory avoidanceNutritional deficiencyNo body image distortion

Pica

Eating

Persistent eating of non-nutritive, non-food substances (dirt, clay, chalk, soap, paper, hair) for at least one month, at a developmental level where it is inappropriate. Can cause serious medical complications.

Key Features
Non-food eatingPersistent 1+ monthMedical risks

Rumination Disorder

Eating

Repeated regurgitation of food over at least one month re-chewing, re-swallowing, or spitting out food that was previously swallowed. Not due to a gastrointestinal condition or another eating disorder.

Key Features
Food regurgitationRe-chewing1+ month duration

Orthorexia Nervosa

Eating (Emerging)

An obsessive focus on "healthy" or "pure" eating that is not yet an official DSM-5 diagnosis but is widely recognised. Preoccupation with food quality impairs social, professional, and nutritional functioning.

Key Features
Food purity obsessionRigid dietary rulesSocial impairment

Sleep-Wake Disorders

Disorders characterised by dissatisfaction with sleep quality, timing, or amount, causing significant distress or impairment.

Insomnia Disorder

Sleep-Wake

Predominant complaint of difficulty initiating sleep, maintaining sleep, or early-morning awakening with inability to return to sleep, occurring at least 3 nights per week for at least 3 months despite adequate opportunity.

Key Features
Difficulty falling asleepNight wakingEarly awakeningDaytime impairment

Hypersomnolence Disorder & Narcolepsy

Sleep-Wake

Hypersomnolence involves excessive daytime sleepiness despite adequate sleep. Narcolepsy adds cataplexy (sudden muscle weakness triggered by emotions), sleep paralysis, and hallucinations due to orexin deficiency.

Key Features
Excessive sleepinessCataplexySleep paralysisHypnagogic hallucinations

Sleep-Related Breathing Disorders

Sleep-Wake

Including Obstructive Sleep Apnea (repeated airway obstruction), Central Sleep Apnea (breathing control failure), and Sleep-Related Hypoventilation. Cause fragmented sleep, snoring, and daytime sleepiness.

Key Features
SnoringApnea episodesDaytime fatigueMorning headaches

Circadian Rhythm Sleep-Wake Disorders

Sleep-Wake

A persistent pattern of sleep disruption due to misalignment between one's internal clock and external environment. Types include Delayed Sleep Phase, Advanced Sleep Phase, Irregular, Non-24-Hour, Shift Work, and Jet Lag types.

Key Features
Misaligned body clockInsomnia at desired timesSleepiness at work/school

Parasomnias

Sleep-Wake

Abnormal behaviours, experiences, or physiological events during sleep or sleep-wake transitions. Includes Sleepwalking, Sleep Terrors, Nightmare Disorder, Sleep Paralysis, REM Sleep Behaviour Disorder, and Exploding Head Syndrome.

Key Features
SleepwalkingNight terrorsREM behaviourVivid nightmares

Restless Legs Syndrome (Willis-Ekbom Disease)

Sleep-Wake

An urge to move the legs (or arms) usually accompanied by uncomfortable, distressing sensations. Worsens with rest and is relieved by movement. Symptoms are worse in the evening and at night, disrupting sleep.

Key Features
Urge to move legsEvening worseningRelief with movement

Sexual Dysfunctions & Gender Dysphoria

Disorders affecting sexual functioning and gender identity distress.

Sexual Dysfunctions

Sexual

A group of conditions including Erectile Disorder, Female Sexual Interest/Arousal Disorder, Female Orgasmic Disorder, Male Hypoactive Sexual Desire, Premature Ejaculation, Delayed Ejaculation, and Genito-Pelvic Pain/Penetration Disorder (vaginismus/dyspareunia).

Key Features
Desire difficultiesArousal problemsOrgasm difficultiesPain during sex

Gender Dysphoria

Gender Identity

Significant distress caused by the incongruence between one's experienced or expressed gender and one's assigned gender. The diagnosis focuses on distress, not on gender identity itself. ICD-11 reclassifies this as "Gender Incongruence" outside mental disorders.

Key Features
Gender incongruenceSignificant distressDesire for different gender

Substance Use & Addictive Disorders

Disorders related to substance misuse and behavioural addictions causing significant impairment.

Alcohol Use Disorder (AUD)

Substance

A problematic pattern of alcohol use leading to clinically significant impairment or distress including tolerance, withdrawal, loss of control, and continued use despite harmful consequences. Ranges from mild to severe.

Key Features
ToleranceWithdrawalLoss of controlCraving

Opioid Use Disorder

Substance

Problematic pattern of opioid use including heroin, morphine, codeine, and prescription pain killers. Characterised by strong cravings, withdrawal (muscle aches, vomiting, anxiety), tolerance, and continued use despite harm.

Key Features
CravingsSevere withdrawalToleranceContinued use despite harm

Stimulant Use Disorder

Substance

Problematic use of cocaine, amphetamines, tik (methamphetamine), and other stimulants. Causes euphoria, hyperactivity, paranoia, and psychosis with heavy use. Withdrawal involves crash, fatigue, and depression.

Key Features
Euphoria seekingParanoiaCrash/withdrawalPsychosis

Cannabis Use Disorder

Substance

Problematic cannabis use causing clinically significant impairment tolerance, withdrawal (irritability, sleep disruption, appetite changes), difficulty cutting down, and continued use despite negative consequences.

Key Features
ToleranceWithdrawalMotivational deficits

Tobacco/Nicotine Use Disorder

Substance

Problematic pattern of tobacco or nicotine use characterised by strong cravings, withdrawal symptoms (irritability, anxiety, difficulty concentrating), and inability to quit despite health consequences.

Key Features
Nicotine cravingsWithdrawalContinued use despite illness

Hallucinogen Use Disorder & HPPD

Substance

Problematic use of LSD, psilocybin mushrooms, mescaline, and similar substances. Hallucinogen Persisting Perception Disorder (HPPD) involves re-experiencing perceptual symptoms (flashbacks) after cessation of use.

Key Features
Perceptual disturbancesFlashbacks (HPPD)Distress

Inhalant Use Disorder

Substance

Problematic use of inhaled substances glue, paint, aerosols, solvents causing intoxication. Can cause permanent brain damage, sudden death, and serious neurological effects even from a single use.

Key Features
Volatile substance useNeurological damageHigh medical risk

Gambling Disorder

Behavioural Addiction

Persistent and recurrent problematic gambling behaviour causing significant impairment preoccupation with gambling, needing to bet increasing amounts, failed attempts to stop, lying about gambling, and continuing despite financial ruin.

Key Features
Gambling preoccupationChasing lossesLyingFinancial harm

Gaming Disorder

Behavioural Addiction

Recognised by ICD-11 as a pattern of persistent or recurrent gaming behaviour characterised by impaired control, increasing priority given to gaming, and continuation despite negative consequences lasting at least 12 months.

Key Features
Loss of gaming controlPriority over other activitiesContinued despite harm

Personality Disorders

Enduring patterns of inner experience and behaviour deviating markedly from cultural expectations, pervasive across situations, leading to distress or impairment.

Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD)

Personality

Pervasive instability of self-image, interpersonal relationships, and emotions. Intense fear of abandonment, impulsivity, chronic emptiness, self-harm, and rapidly shifting moods. ICD-11 describes this as "Emotionally Unstable PD."

Key Features
Fear of abandonmentUnstable relationshipsSelf-harmIdentity disturbance

Narcissistic Personality Disorder (NPD)

Personality

Pervasive pattern of grandiosity, need for admiration, and lack of empathy. Sense of entitlement, exploitative behaviour, envy of others, and arrogance. Underlying fragile self-esteem despite outward confidence.

Key Features
GrandiosityEntitlementLack of empathyAdmiration-seeking

Antisocial Personality Disorder (ASPD)

Personality

Pervasive pattern of disregard for and violation of others' rights deceitfulness, impulsivity, aggressiveness, reckless disregard for safety, irresponsibility, and lack of remorse. Requires history of Conduct Disorder before age 15.

Key Features
DeceitfulnessImpulsivityAggressionLack of remorse

Avoidant Personality Disorder (AvPD)

Personality

Pervasive pattern of social inhibition, feelings of inadequacy, and extreme sensitivity to negative evaluation. Avoids occupational activities involving interpersonal contact and is reluctant to engage unless certain of being liked.

Key Features
Social avoidanceFear of rejectionLow self-image

Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder (OCPD)

Personality

Preoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, and mental and interpersonal control at the expense of flexibility, openness, and efficiency. Distinct from OCD symptoms are ego-syntonic (feel right to the person).

Key Features
PerfectionismRigidityHoarding tendenciesWorkaholism

Paranoid Personality Disorder

Personality

Pervasive distrust and suspiciousness of others such that their motives are interpreted as malevolent. Suspects exploitation, is reluctant to confide in others, reads hidden meanings into events, and bears grudges persistently.

Key Features
Pervasive distrustSuspiciousGrudge-bearing

Schizoid Personality Disorder

Personality

Pervasive pattern of detachment from social relationships and restricted range of emotional expression. Little desire for close relationships, chooses solitary activities, appears indifferent to praise or criticism.

Key Features
Social detachmentEmotional coldnessSolitary lifestyle

Schizotypal Personality Disorder

Personality

Pervasive pattern of social and interpersonal deficits marked by acute discomfort with close relationships, eccentric behaviour, and cognitive or perceptual distortions magical thinking, ideas of reference, unusual perceptions.

Key Features
Magical thinkingIdeas of referenceEccentric behaviour

Dependent Personality Disorder

Personality

Excessive need to be taken care of, leading to submissive and clinging behaviour and fear of separation. Difficulty making decisions without excessive advice, difficulty disagreeing for fear of loss of support.

Key Features
Difficulty deciding aloneFear of abandonmentSubmissive behaviour

Histrionic Personality Disorder

Personality

Pervasive pattern of excessive emotionality and attention-seeking behaviour uncomfortable when not the centre of attention, sexually provocative, theatrical, easily influenced, and considers relationships more intimate than they are.

Key Features
Attention-seekingDramaticSuggestibleShallow emotions

Neurocognitive Disorders

Disorders characterised by a decline in cognitive function from a previously attained level.

Major Neurocognitive Disorder (Dementia)

Neurocognitive

Significant cognitive decline affecting one or more cognitive domains memory, language, perception, social cognition, attention, or executive function severe enough to interfere with independence. Includes Alzheimer's disease, vascular dementia, Lewy body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, and others.

Key Features
Memory lossLanguage difficultiesLoss of independenceExecutive dysfunction

Mild Neurocognitive Disorder (MCI)

Neurocognitive

Modest cognitive decline from a previous level of performance in one or more cognitive domains that does not interfere with independence in everyday activities. May represent an early stage of dementia in some cases.

Key Features
Modest cognitive declineIndependence preservedSubjective memory concerns

Delirium

Neurocognitive

Acute disturbance in attention and awareness developing over a short period (hours to days), fluctuating in severity, accompanied by additional cognitive disturbance. Always has an underlying medical cause infections, medications, surgery.

Key Features
Acute confusionFluctuating consciousnessMedical causeDisorientation

Disruptive, Impulse-Control & Childhood Behavioural Disorders

Disorders involving problems with emotional and behavioural self-control, primarily affecting children and adolescents.

Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD)

Disruptive

Pattern of angry/irritable mood, argumentative/defiant behaviour, and vindictiveness lasting at least 6 months directed at authority figures. Common in children and adolescents, often preceding conduct disorder.

Key Features
DefianceIrritabilityArgumentativenessVindictiveness

Conduct Disorder

Disruptive

Repetitive and persistent pattern of behaviour violating the basic rights of others or major age-appropriate societal norms aggression to people/animals, property destruction, deceitfulness, theft, and serious rule violations.

Key Features
AggressionRule violationsProperty destructionDeceitfulness

Intermittent Explosive Disorder (IED)

Impulse-Control

Recurrent, significant impulsive outbursts of aggression verbal or physical that are grossly disproportionate to the situation and not better explained by another disorder. Often described as "road rage," anger attacks, or domestic violence.

Key Features
Explosive outburstsDisproportionate angerRemorse after

Kleptomania

Impulse-Control

Recurrent failure to resist impulses to steal objects not needed for personal use or monetary value. Rising tension before the theft, gratification during, and often guilt or shame after. Not motivated by anger or delusions.

Key Features
Recurrent stealingNot for monetary gainTension then relief

Pyromania

Impulse-Control

Deliberate and purposeful fire-setting on more than one occasion, preceded by tension or affective arousal. Fascination with fire and its context. Gratification when setting or witnessing fires. Not for monetary gain or ideology.

Key Features
Repeated fire-settingFire fascinationTension beforeRelief after

Elimination Disorders (Enuresis & Encopresis)

Childhood

Enuresis: repeated voiding of urine in bed or clothes in children old enough for continence. Encopresis: repeated passage of faeces in inappropriate places. Both cause significant distress and are often associated with other developmental issues.

Key Features
BedwettingFaecal incontinenceAge-inappropriate

Paraphilic Disorders

Intense and persistent sexual interests in atypical objects, situations, or individuals that cause distress or involve harm to others.

Voyeuristic Disorder

Paraphilic

Intense and recurrent sexual arousal from observing an unsuspecting person who is naked, undressing, or engaging in sexual activity. The person has acted on these urges or is markedly distressed by them.

Key Features
Non-consensual observationRecurrent urgesDistress or harm

Exhibitionistic Disorder

Paraphilic

Intense and recurrent sexual arousal from exposing one's genitals to an unsuspecting person. The person has acted on these urges or is markedly distressed.

Key Features
Genital exposureUnsuspecting victimRecurrent

Frotteuristic Disorder

Paraphilic

Intense and recurrent sexual arousal from touching or rubbing against a non-consenting person. Typically occurs in crowded places. The person has acted on these urges or is markedly distressed.

Key Features
Touching non-consentingCrowded settings

Sexual Masochism & Sadism Disorder

Paraphilic

Sexual Masochism Disorder: arousal from being humiliated, beaten, or made to suffer. Sexual Sadism Disorder: arousal from inflicting suffering on others. Only a disorder if causing distress or involving non-consenting persons.

Key Features
Pain/humiliation arousalDistress or non-consent

Pedophilic Disorder

Paraphilic

Intense and recurrent sexual urges or fantasies involving prepubescent children (generally 13 years or younger). The person has acted on these urges or is markedly distressed. Any sexual act with a child is a crime regardless of diagnosis.

Key Features
Sexual interest in childrenCriminal behaviourRecurrent urges

Fetishistic Disorder

Paraphilic

Intense and recurrent sexual arousal from non-living objects or highly specific focus on non-genital body parts, causing clinically significant distress or impairment. The paraphilia itself (fetish) is only a disorder if causing harm or distress.

Key Features
Object/body part focusDistress or impairment

Transvestic Disorder

Paraphilic

Intense and recurrent sexual arousal from cross-dressing (wearing clothing associated with a different gender), causing clinically significant distress or impairment. Distinct from gender dysphoria and from non-sexual cross-dressing.

Key Features
Cross-dressing arousalSignificant distress
