Why We Speak Out

Our Commitment to Ending GBV

South Africa has one of the highest rates of gender-based violence in the world. Every 3 hours a woman is killed by an intimate partner. Every 17 seconds a woman is raped. These are not just statistics — they are mothers, daughters, sisters, friends, and sons.

Journey4Change believes that awareness is the first act of protection. We travel across all 9 provinces sharing stories, connecting communities to resources, and giving survivors a platform to speak — because silence is what violence feeds on.

No one should live in fear in their own home. No one should be told they are to blame. No one should suffer in silence. We are here, and we are walking with you.

Understanding GBV

Forms of Gender-Based Violence

GBV is not only physical. Recognising all its forms is the first step to understanding, escaping, and healing from it.

Physical Violence

Any act that causes bodily harm — hitting, kicking, slapping, choking, burning, or using weapons. Physical abuse often escalates over time and can be fatal.

Emotional & Psychological

Constant criticism, humiliation, threats, isolation from loved ones, gaslighting, and controlling behaviour that destroys a person's sense of self-worth.

Sexual Violence

Any sexual act without full and free consent — including rape, sexual assault, coercion, and marital rape. Consent can be withdrawn at any time.

Economic Abuse

Controlling access to money, preventing employment, taking wages, destroying property, or creating financial dependency to trap a person in a relationship.

Digital & Online Abuse

Harassment, stalking, non-consensual sharing of images, monitoring devices, and using social media to control, humiliate, or threaten a partner.

Forced Marriage & Trafficking

Forcing someone into marriage against their will, or trafficking and exploiting people for labour or sexual purposes. Both are serious crimes in South Africa.

Know the Signs

Warning Signs of an Abusive Relationship

Abuse rarely starts with violence. It begins with control. Recognising these signs could save a life.

01

Extreme Jealousy and Possessiveness

Treating jealousy as love, checking your phone, demanding to know where you are at all times, and getting angry when you spend time with others.

02

Isolation from Friends & Family

Gradually cutting you off from your support network, creating conflict with your loved ones, or controlling who you see and speak to.

03

Controlling Finances

Demanding access to your bank accounts, giving you an "allowance," preventing you from working, or making you account for every cent spent.

04

Threats and Intimidation

Threatening to harm you, your children, your family, or pets. Threatening to report you to authorities, destroy your reputation, or take your children away.

05

Blame-Shifting

Making you feel responsible for their anger and violence — "You made me do this," "You know how to push my buttons," or "You deserve this."

06

Rapid Escalation

Pushing for a very serious commitment very quickly — moving in together, engagement, or pregnancy — before you know them well.

07

Minimising and Denying

Telling you the abuse did not happen, saying you are too sensitive, denying incidents of violence, or laughing off serious behaviour.

08

Monitoring and Tracking

Checking your location, reading your messages, installing tracking apps without consent, showing up unannounced, or questioning every interaction.

GBV Helplines & Resources

All services below are free, confidential, and available to anyone experiencing or witnessing GBV — regardless of gender.

GBV Command Centre

Government 24/7 national helpline for GBV survivors. Access to shelters, legal advice, social workers, and emergency response.

0800 150 150

Stop Gender Violence

A dedicated national helpline providing counselling, referrals to shelters, and legal assistance for GBV survivors across South Africa.

0800 428 428
Free · Confidential

Lifeline South Africa

24/7 crisis counselling for GBV, trauma, abuse, and suicidal thoughts. Trained counsellors. Confidential and free of charge.

0800 011 1
Free · 24/7 · lifelinesa.co.za

Thuthuzela Care Centres

One-stop facilities at hospitals offering medical care, counselling, and police services for rape and sexual assault survivors. No need to visit multiple locations.

Be Prepared

Your Safety Plan

A safety plan is a personalised set of steps that help protect you when violence is happening or might happen. Having a plan can save your life.

Safety Planning

If You Are in Danger Right Now

You cannot always predict when violence will occur — but you can prepare. Work through these steps with a counsellor or trusted person.

1
Identify a Safe Place

Know where you can go if you need to leave quickly — a trusted neighbour, family member, church, shelter, or public place.

2
Pack an Emergency Bag

Keep a bag ready (or with someone you trust) with: ID, birth certificates, cash, medications, phone charger, a change of clothes, and important documents.

3
Memorise Key Numbers

Know at least two phone numbers by heart — a trusted person and a helpline — in case your phone is taken or broken.

4
Tell Someone You Trust

Tell a trusted friend, family member, or neighbour what is happening. Ask them to call the police if they hear or see something concerning.

5
Know Your Rights

You have the right to apply for a Protection Order at any Magistrate's Court. It is free, and violating it is a criminal offence in South Africa.

Be an Ally

How You Can Help

You do not have to be a survivor to take action. Everyone in the community has a role to play in ending GBV.

Listen Without Judgement

If someone discloses abuse to you, believe them. Do not ask why they did not leave sooner. Simply say: "I believe you. I am here. You are not alone."

Speak Up and Speak Out

Challenge GBV-enabling language, jokes, and attitudes in your home, workplace, and community. Silence is complicity — your voice matters.

Support Survivors Practically

Help them access resources, accompany them to the police or Thuthuzela Centre if they wish, or simply be consistently present and check in regularly.

Teach the Next Generation

Talk to children about consent, respect, healthy relationships, and equality. Prevention begins with education — at home, at school, in the community.

Support GBV Organisations

Donate to or volunteer with organisations fighting GBV. Even a small contribution to shelters, legal funds, or awareness campaigns makes a real difference.

Hold Men Accountable

Men — challenge each other. Do not let harmful behaviour slide in your circle. Being an ally means being willing to have uncomfortable conversations with friends and family.
